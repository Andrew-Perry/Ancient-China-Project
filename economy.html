Silk Road

The silk road was a major source of income for Ancient China. It was a network of trade routes that was officially created during the Han Dynasty. The reason the trade network was called the silk road was because silk was one of the major exports from China that was in high demand from other nations at the time. A few other exports were tea, dyes, precious stones, china, spices, rice, paper, and gunpowder. All of which were highly sought after by other countries and resulted in China becoming richer than most of the world. The network was widely used until the Ottoman Empire boycotted trade and ended up closing the routes.




The Yuanbao/Currency

The Yuanbo was the main form of currency in Ancient China. It was a circular coin that was made from copper, iron, lead, gold, and silver with different shapes and marks. Eventually the Yuanbo was left behind as in the year 960 CE China switched to a paper currency which was the first in the world to do so. An uncommon belief for the Ancient Chinese monetary system was that it was merely a means of exchange and a measure of value alone. The Chinese didn’t believe that money was a way to store value, which is why it remained low in value for a long time.




The Invention of the Compass

The Chinese discovered magnetic properties of objects, and this discovery has played a very large role in the development of science and technology. This was an important technological advancement because it allowed for better navigation during travel, and during earlier times was used to align buildings making it a very useful tool for architecture. The invention of the compass is seen as one of the four great inventions of ancient China along with gunpowder, paper, and printing. The picture shown is of a very basic compass created by shaping a lodestone into a spoon and placing it on a flat bronze plate.The handle of the spoon when placed would always point south, and the Chinese harnessed this discovery and used it to their advantage.






The Development of Farming

In Ancient China, before the development of a large central government, the people lived in small farming settlements. The picture on the right displays one of these farm areas with few people and little technological advancement. This was their main source of income, and they would trade amongst themselves with their crops. Much later in China’s history, during the time of the Song dynasty, much more farmland was created, and with the improvements in farm tools and fertilizer, the economy was boosted from the high amount of food being produced. In order to feed all of the people in the cities, the majority of China’s population had to be farmers. One main improvement in the farming system was water reading and irrigation.
